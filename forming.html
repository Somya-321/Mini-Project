Sheet metal forming is a major technological advancement of the twentieth century, allowing efficient mass production of complex shapes from flat metal sheets. This process involves stretching and shrinking the material in three directions to achieve the final form. Various classification systems have been developed to organize the wide variety of formed shapes, such as Sachs' method, which groups parts based on their contours. In contrast to bulk forming, sheet forming primarily uses tensile forces in the plane of the sheet, avoiding compression to prevent defects like wrinkling or buckling. Maintaining sheet thickness is important to avoid failure during forming.

Classification of Forming Process

  1. Classification based on Tooling:

    2. Manual Forming:

    Hand forming is a traditional method used to remove wrinkles that may be left by machine forming. It is typically applied in situations where only a few identical parts are needed or during experimental work. This process involves manual intervention, making it ideal for low-volume production or when fine adjustments are required that machines cannot easily handle.

    Press Forming:

    Press forming is a high-volume sheet metal forming process performed using mechanical or hydraulic presses. Mechanical presses are fast with short strokes, while hydraulic presses operate slower but provide longer strokes for complex shapes. Presses are classified as single-action , double-action , or triple-action based on the number of slides. Single-action presses have one vertical slide, double-action adds a hold-down slide for deep drawing, and triple-action offers added control for intricate operations.

    Key tools include a convex punch and concave die, requiring precise alignment. Progressive, compound, and transfer dies enhance efficiency by performing multiple operations in one setup. Clamping pressure prevents wrinkling, applied via hold-down rings or springs. A press brake, a type of single-action press with a long, narrow bed, is used for creating accurate straight bends in sheet metal.

    Rubber Pad Forming:

    Rubber Pad Forming (RPF) is a metal forming technique in which a sheet metal blank is pressed between a rigid die and a flexible rubber pad, typically made of polyurethane. During the process, applied pressure causes the rubber to deform, forcing the sheet metal into the die cavity and allowing it to take the desired shape. The rubber pad may have a standard membrane-like form for general use or may be specifically machined to replicate the contour of a die or punch, depending on the application requirements.

    Explosive Forming:

    Explosive forming is particularly suited for producing large parts in low production lot sizes. In this process, a sheet-metal blank is placed over a die cavity, and an explosive charge deforms the blank using a shockwave as a frictionless punch. This technique is useful for creating large, complex parts that would be difficult to form using conventional methods, offering both speed and precision in low-volume production.

  2. Classification Based on Material Flow

    3. Bending and Contouring:

    Bending and contouring involve various techniques to shape metal into cylindrical or conical forms. Bending rolls are used to form such shapes, with a third roll sometimes employed to prevent buckling in thin-gauge sheets. Wiper-type equipment ensures uniform deformation along the length of the part. Additionally, wrap forming applies both compression and tensile stress to prevent buckling and achieve the desired contour, making it effective for producing complex shapes.

    Spinning:

    Spinning is a process used to create deep, circular-symmetric parts such as tank heads and television cones. In this method, the metal is clamped onto a rotating spindle and progressively formed into shape. Shear-spinning is a variant that reduces part thickness, while tube spinning focuses on reducing the wall thickness of tubes. Spinning is ideal for creating parts with a high degree of symmetry and precision.

    Stretch Forming

    Stretch forming is a sheet metal forming process in which the material is plastically deformed by the application of primarily tensile forces, causing it to stretch over a form block or die. The process evolved from stretcher-leveling techniques used in rolled sheet metal. Stretch forming is widely employed in the aerospace industry to fabricate components with large radii of curvature, often involving double curvature geometries.

    The equipment consists of a vertical hydraulic ram with a punch and gripping jaws. The sheet is first draped over the form block with light tension, then plastically stretched to its final shape. Unlike wrap forming, the material is first positioned before full tension is applied.

  3. Classification based on Purpose

    • Cutting operations

      • Shearing:

      Shearing is a cutting process that separates sheet metal using two blades. During the process, a narrow region of the material undergoes plastic deformation until fracture initiates at the blade interfaces and propagates inward, ultimately resulting in material separation.

      The depth of punch penetration prior to fracture is governed by the ductility of the material, being relatively shallow for brittle materials and potentially exceeding the sheet thickness in the case of highly ductile metals.

      Proper blade clearance is critical for a clean shear. Ideal clearance allows fractures to meet at the center, minimizing edge distortion. Insufficient clearance increases energy requirements and causes ragged edges, while excessive clearance leads to burr formation and edge distortion. Clearances typically range from 2% to 10% of the sheet thickness, increasing with thickness. Burr height also rises with both clearance and ductility.

      Blanking : In blanking, a closed contour is sheared from the sheet, and the removed portion becomes the desired component. This process is commonly used for producing flat parts like washers and discs.

      Punching or piercing removes material within a closed contour, where the discarded inner piece is not used.

      Notching involves shearing along the edge of the sheet, usually to create space or clearance in a component.

      Parting refers to simultaneous shearing along at least two lines, allowing the separation of multiple components from a single sheet in a balanced manner.

      Slitting is a straight-line shearing operation that does not remove material but divides the sheet into narrower strips, often used for coil or roll processing.

      Trimming is employed as a finishing operation, where excess material around the periphery of a formed part is removed to achieve precise dimensions. It is often used after forging or forming operations.

      Shaving further refines the sheared edge by removing a thin layer of material, ensuring smoother and more accurate edgesâ€”particularly useful when high precision is required.

      Fine Blanking is a precise shearing process used to produce small, intricate parts like gears and levers with smooth, square edges.     • Bending operations

      • Bending is a fundamental sheet metal forming process that transforms a straight length into a curved shape. It is extensively employed in manufacturing operations to produce components such as channels, drums, and tanks. During bending, the inner surface undergoes compression while the outer surface is in tension, with the neutral axis shifting depending on the extent of plastic deformation. A decrease in thickness at the bend occurs to maintain volume consistency.

    • Drawing operations

      • Shallow Drawing

      Shallow drawing is a metal forming process characterized by the creation of components with relatively shallow depths. It involves stretching and compressing sheet metal to meet specific design requirements, making it suitable for a wide range of materials. The process employs a punch and die to shape the metal, while the edges of the blank are restrained to ensure precision and uniformity during deformation. A part is typically classified as shallow-drawn when the depth of the draw is less than the smallest dimension of the opening or less than half the diameter of the part. Shallow drawing is commonly used across various industries due to its efficiency and versatility, including aerospace, energy storage, medical device manufacturing, military and defense, oil and gas, and semiconductor sectors

      Deep Drawing

      Deep drawing is a metal forming process wherein a flat sheet (blank) is formed into a cup-like shape by being pressed into a die using a punch. Common applications include automotive panels, shell casings, cans, sinks, and various housings. The process involves radially drawing a sheet metal blank into a die cavity using a punch.

      A blank-holder is applied to control material flow and prevent wrinkling. As the punch descends, it shapes the workpiece into the desired form, typically matching the punch geometry.

      The process is classified as deep drawing when the depth of the formed part exceeds its diameter.

Forming Limit Diagram

The Forming Limit Diagram (FLD), introduced by Keeler and Goodwin, is an experimental tool used to assess the formability of sheet metals. They represent the combinations of major and minor strains at which local necking begins, which is typically considered a failure in forming processes, even though actual cracking may occur later. They are most reliable under proportional loading

FLDs are plotted on a graph where the vertical axis shows major strain (always positive) and the horizontal axis shows minor strain (which can be positive or negative). These strains are measured by applying a grid (circular, linear, or random patterns) on the sheet metal surface before deformation. As the sheet is strained, the grid deforms, allowing strain measurement. The diagram helps identify safe and failure zones in forming operations, enabling better design and material selection.

Material factors influencing FLD include failure strain, strain hardening, and anisotropy. Higher failure strain and normal anisotropy improve formability, while strain hardening helps distribute strain more evenly in stretching. Non-material factors like stress ratios and lubrication also affect formability by reducing peak strains and improving strain distribution.

Defects in Formed Sheet-Metal Parts

Sheet-metal forming processes can produce various defects that affect the quality, appearance, and structural integrity of the final product. These defects arise due to material behavior, forming conditions, or process parameters.

  1. Cracking and Necking

    2. The most severe defect is cracking, which compromises the structural integrity of the component. Local necking or excessive thinning may precede cracking and also render the part unusable. These defects usually occur due to overstressing the material beyond its ductile limits.

  2. Thinning and Fracture in Deep Drawing

    3. In deep drawing, the most common defect is the separation of the cup bottom from the wall, typically near the punch radius, where thinning is most severe. This can be minimized by:

    • Using a larger punch radius
    • Reducing the punch load

    Radial cracks in the flange or cup edge indicate insufficient ductility, especially during redrawing without annealing, where circumferential shrinkage is high.

  3. Wrinkling

    4. Wrinkling occurs due to circumferential compressive stresses that cause the sheet to buckle, especially in the flange area. It can be analyzed as column buckling under compression. To prevent wrinkling:

    • Avoid using oversized blanks.
    • Apply sufficient blank-holder pressure to suppress buckling.
    • Use thicker sheets with higher buckling resistance.

  4. Springback and Dimensional Inaccuracy

    5. Springback occurs when the sheet partially returns to its original shape after forming due to elastic recovery, leading to dimensional inaccuracies. This must be compensated for in tool design or forming parameters.

  5. Surface Defects

    1. Orange Peel

      2. This defect appears as surface roughness in heavily deformed regions, typically due to large grain size. It results from uneven deformation of individual grains and can be mitigated by using fine-grained sheet metal.

    2. Stretcher Strains ("Worms")

      3. Common in low-carbon steels, these appear as flame-like surface depressions due to yield-point elongation. To eliminate them:

      • Apply temper-rolling (1/2â€“2% reduction) to remove the yield point.
      • Be aware that strain aging during storage may reintroduce the defect.

  6. Directionality and Anisotropy

    7. Rolling and other forming processes introduce mechanical and crystallographic anisotropy, affecting formability. For example, bending is harder along the rolling direction than across it. Preferred crystallographic orientations influence behavior during forming.

  7. Earing

    8. Earing refers to the formation of wavy edges on the top rim of a deep-drawn cup due to planar anisotropy. It increases material waste due to trimming requirements.
